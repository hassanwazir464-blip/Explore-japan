Food & Culture

Experience the flavours and traditions of Japan

Japanese Food

Japanese cuisine is known all over the world for its quality, variety, and presentation. Japan has more Michelin-starred restaurants than any other country in the world. Whether you are eating a simple bowl of ramen or an elaborate sushi feast, food in Japan is almost always an incredible experience.

Sushi and Sashimi

Sushi is probably the most well-known Japanese food outside of Japan. It consists of vinegared rice topped with fresh fish, seafood, or vegetables. Sashimi is similar but without the rice, just slices of very fresh raw fish.

In Japan you can eat sushi at a wide range of places, from high-end restaurants to affordable kaiten-zushi (conveyor belt sushi) restaurants where plates pass by on a moving belt and you take what you want. The quality is generally very high even at cheaper restaurants.

Ramen

Ramen is a type of Japanese noodle soup that has become popular worldwide. There are many different regional styles of ramen in Japan, each with its own unique broth and toppings. The four main types are tonkotsu (pork bone broth), shoyu (soy sauce broth), miso broth, and shio (salt broth).

Ramen shops can be found on almost every street in Japan and a decent bowl typically costs between 800 and 1500 yen. It is the perfect meal after a long day of sightseeing.

Other Foods to Try

Tempura

Lightly battered and deep fried vegetables or seafood, served with a dipping sauce. Simple but absolutely delicious.

Matcha

Finely ground green tea powder used in drinks, ice cream, and sweets. A uniquely Japanese flavour that is very popular with tourists.

Yakitori

Grilled chicken skewers cooked over charcoal, often seasoned with soy sauce and mirin. Found at festivals and small grill restaurants called izakayas.

Japanese Culture

Japan has a rich and unique culture that is unlike anywhere else in the world. Understanding some basic cultural customs will help you get the most out of your visit and show respect to the local people.

Temples and Shrines

Japan has two main types of religious sites: Shinto shrines and Buddhist temples. Shrines are dedicated to Shinto gods and are usually marked by a torii gate at the entrance. Temples are Buddhist and often have a pagoda and incense burners.

When visiting these places it is important to be respectful and quiet. At many shrines there is a purification fountain where you wash your hands before entering. Dress modestly and avoid loud behaviour.

Cultural Tips: In Japan it is considered rude to eat or drink while walking. You should also remove your shoes before entering someone's home or certain traditional restaurants. Tipping is not part of Japanese culture and can even be considered offensive in some situations.
